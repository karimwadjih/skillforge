# Roadmap Tradeoff Mapper

## Summary

Compare competing roadmap bets with explicit upside, delay cost, dependency drag, and a forced sequence.

## When To Use

- Two or more roadmap bets are competing for the same quarter and every argument sounds plausible in isolation.
- You need a tradeoff map with upside, downside, dependency load, timing cost, and a recommended sequence instead of another ranking ritual.
- The organization repeatedly revisits the same priority fights and needs a reusable comparison posture.

## Avoid When

- The team still lacks credible options worth comparing.
- The decision is primarily a budget allocation exercise with little product or dependency tradeoff to reason about.

## Inputs

- candidate roadmap options
- business or product goals
- constraints and capacity assumptions
- dependencies and timing risks
- known evidence for each option

## Output Contract

- tradeoff map
- option-by-option comparison
- recommended sequence or cut line
- open evidence gaps

## Workflow

1. Confirm the decision context, audience, and missing inputs.
2. Build a roadmap tradeoff map with upside, downside, dependency load, delay cost, and a recommended sequence or cut line using the available evidence and constraints.
3. Separate facts from assumptions and make confidence explicit.
4. Finish with next actions, open questions, or escalation notes.

## Deliverable Shape

A roadmap tradeoff map with upside, downside, dependency load, delay cost, and a recommended sequence or cut line

## Guardrails

- Do not invent facts that are not present in the supplied materials.
- Make uncertainty explicit when inputs are incomplete or contradictory.
- Keep the output structured enough that another operator can reuse it.
