# Literature Map

## Summary

Map a crowded topic into schools of thought, evidence weight, and the shortest smart reading path.

## When To Use

- You need a fast orientation before a team spends a week reading the wrong sources.
- You need a literature map with theme clusters, representative sources, disputed questions, evidence weight, and a reading order instead of a citation dump.
- The work repeats often enough that the reading strategy itself should become reusable.

## Avoid When

- You need a formal systematic review with explicit inclusion and exclusion methodology.
- The answer depends on fresh experiments, interviews, or unpublished internal data rather than existing literature.

## Inputs

- framing question or decision the reading should support
- seed bibliography, search results, or must-read source set
- time window or domain boundary
- constraints on depth, rigor, or turnaround time

## Output Contract

- literature landscape map
- theme clusters and fault lines
- evidence-strength notes
- recommended reading path

## Workflow

1. Confirm the decision context, audience, and missing inputs.
2. Build a literature map with theme clusters, representative sources, evidence weight, disputed questions, and a recommended reading path using the available evidence and constraints.
3. Separate facts from assumptions and make confidence explicit.
4. Finish with next actions, open questions, or escalation notes.

## Deliverable Shape

A literature map with theme clusters, representative sources, evidence weight, disputed questions, and a recommended reading path

## Guardrails

- Do not invent facts that are not present in the supplied materials.
- Make uncertainty explicit when inputs are incomplete or contradictory.
- Keep the output structured enough that another operator can reuse it.
