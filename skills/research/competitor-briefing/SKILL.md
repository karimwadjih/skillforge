# Competitor Briefing

## Summary

Turn competitor noise into a briefing about intent, durability, and what your team should ignore.

## When To Use

- A competitor announcement, launch, or pricing move is creating internal panic or overreaction.
- You need a briefing with signal quality, likely intent, customer relevance, and recommended response posture instead of a feature checklist.
- The workflow repeats often enough that the organization benefits from a reusable competitor-reading posture.

## Avoid When

- You need a formal market sizing exercise or a full strategic planning document.
- There is not enough evidence to distinguish signal from rumor and the right move is to wait for more information.

## Inputs

- competitor signal set such as launches, pricing changes, win-loss notes, or field reports
- your current product or GTM context
- known customer or segment overlap
- decision horizon for response

## Output Contract

- competitor briefing
- signal-confidence notes
- likely strategic intent
- recommended internal response options

## Workflow

1. Confirm the decision context, audience, and missing inputs.
2. Build a competitor briefing with signal quality, likely strategic intent, customer relevance, and recommended response posture using the available evidence and constraints.
3. Separate facts from assumptions and make confidence explicit.
4. Finish with next actions, open questions, or escalation notes.

## Deliverable Shape

A competitor briefing with signal quality, likely strategic intent, customer relevance, and recommended response posture

## Guardrails

- Do not invent facts that are not present in the supplied materials.
- Make uncertainty explicit when inputs are incomplete or contradictory.
- Keep the output structured enough that another operator can reuse it.
