# Examples

## Pricing move triggers internal panic

### Scenario

A direct competitor launches an aggressive entry-level plan and multiple leaders want an immediate response before renewal conversations start slipping.

### Prompt

Create a competitor briefing on the new pricing move that separates durable strategic signal from launch theater and recommends what our team should change now, later, or not at all.

### Strong Response Should

- Distinguish customer-relevant signal from headline-level noise.
- Explain what the move suggests about intent, not just what changed on the pricing page.
- Recommend a response posture that includes the option to do nothing.

## Security narrative shift

### Scenario

A competitor is suddenly leading with governance and auditability in enterprise deals, and the field team wants to know whether this is messaging, product, or segmentation strategy.

### Prompt

Brief the team on the competitor shift toward governance messaging and spell out whether this changes positioning, product requirements, or enablement priorities for the next quarter.

### Strong Response Should

- Separate packaging and narrative changes from substantive product movement.
- Tie the competitor move to likely buyer segments and sales situations.
- Surface the internal teams that would need to react if the move proves durable.

## Acquisition signal read

### Scenario

A competitor acquires a workflow tool that overlaps partially with the roadmap, and leadership wants a grounded read before reprioritizing.

### Prompt

Produce a competitor briefing on the acquisition with the likely strategic intent, integration uncertainty, customer relevance, and the minimum response worth considering.

### Strong Response Should

- Keep uncertainty explicit around integration timing and execution risk.
- Avoid treating the acquisition headline as immediate product reality.
- Show the narrowest credible internal response instead of defaulting to roadmap churn.
